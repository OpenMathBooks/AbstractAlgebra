First, find rational roots. They are -2 and 7. Dividing we get

\begin{equation*} (x+2)(x-7)(x^6+1) \end{equation*}

The last term has no roots but is still not irreducible. We can use sum of cubes:

\begin{equation*} (x+2)(x-7)(x^2+1)(x^4-x^2 + 1) \end{equation*}

The last degree 4 polynomial is irreducible - use the quadratic formula for \(x^2\) to see that there are no quadratic factors over \(\Q\text{.}\) Now let's factor \((x^6 + 1)\) over \(\bC\text{.}\) There are 6 roots, namely

\begin{equation*} e^{i\pi/6},~~ e^{i3\pi/6}, ~~e^{i5\pi/6},~~ e^{i7\pi/6}, ~~ e^{i9\pi/6}, ~~e^{i11\pi/6} \end{equation*}

These are all “nice” angles, so it is easy to convert back to rectangular form. So over \(\bC\) we have the factorization

\begin{equation*} (x+2)(x-7)(x-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}-\frac{1}{2}i)(x-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}+\frac{1}{2}i)(x-i)(x+i)(x+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}-\frac{1}{2}i)(x+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}+\frac{1}{2}i) \end{equation*}

Finally, to get the factorization over \(\R\) we multiply conjugate pairs:

\begin{equation*} (x+2)(x-7)(x^2+1)(x^2 - \sqrt{3}+1)(x^2 + \sqrt{3}x + 1) \end{equation*}
