Exercise 1.

Previously, we saw that the field \(E = \Q(\sqrt[4]{3},i)\) was the splitting field for the polynomial \(x^4-3\) and that the Galois group \(G(E/\Q)\) was isomorphic to \(D_4\text{.}\) Now let's explore the solvability of the Galois group and whether the polynomial \(x^4 - 3\) is solvable by radicals.

  1. Verify that \(D_4\) (i.e., the Galois group) is solvable. You have done everything you need for this already, you just need to put the pieces together with the definition of solvable.

  2. Pick a subnormal series that witnesses that \(D_4\) is solvable, and look at the corresponding series of subfields of \(E\) (i.e., the subfields that are fixfields for the subgroups). Verify that each of these are normal field extensions (i.e., that they are themselves splitting fields).

  3. Let \(F\) be one of the fixfields from the previous part. What are the elements of the Galois group \(G(F/\Q)\) and how do these relate to the Galois groups \(G(E/\Q)\) and \(G(E/F)\text{?}\) Illustrate the connection using example elements.

  4. Pick a non-normal subgroup of \(D_4\) and consider its fixfield. Demonstrate that the fixfield is not a normal extension.
