Solution 4.3.1.1.

The possible kernels are the subgroups of \(\Z_{24}\text{.}\) We could have \(K = \Z_{24}\text{,}\) and this gives the trivial homomorphism (everything goes to 0). Our next possible kernel is \(\langle 2 \rangle = \{0, 2, 4, \ldots, 22\}\text{.}\) We can then send everything else to \(9 \in \Z_{18}\text{.}\)

Now take \(K = \langle 3 \rangle = \{0, 3, 6, \ldots, 21\}\text{.}\) We know that \(\Z_{24}/K \cong H\) where \(H\) is the image of the homomorphism. So we must have a 3-element subgroup of \(\Z_{18}\) as the image of the homomorphism. Send \(1\) to \(6\) and \(2\) to 12, and so on.

There is not a subgroup of \(\Z_{18}\) containing 4 elements (since \(4\) does not divide 18), so it is impossible for us to have a kernel \(\langle 4 \rangle\text{.}\) The next possible subgroup the kernel could be is \(\langle 6 \rangle = \{0, 6, 12, 18\}\text{,}\) which would give us 6 cosets. There is a subgroup of order 6 in \(\Z_{18}\text{,}\) namely \(\langle 3 \rangle = \{0, 3, 6, 9, 12, 15\}\text{.}\) So we can define \(\phi(n) = 3n\) as the homomorphism.

The kernel \(K = \langle 8 \rangle\) is not possible, nor is \(K = \langle 12 \rangle\text{.}\) Thus we have all of the homomorphisms described.
