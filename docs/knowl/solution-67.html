Solution 6.45.1.

Note that the rational roots theorem tells us that the only possible rational roots are \(\pm 1\text{,}\) \(\pm 2\text{,}\) \(\pm 3\) and \(\pm 6\text{.}\) Plugging these in, we see quickly that \(-1\) and \(3\) are roots.

After dividing by \((x+1)(x-3)\) we are left with \((x^7 - 2)\text{,}\) which is irreducible over \(\mathbb Q\) by Eisenstein.

Now letâ€™s work over \(\mathbb C\text{.}\) We can find the roots of \(x^7 - 2\) by writing \(2 = 2e^{i0} = 2e^{i2\pi} = 2e^{i4\pi} = \cdots = 2e^{i12\pi}\text{.}\)

Taking \(7\)th roots:

\begin{equation*} x = \sqrt[7]{2}, x = \sqrt[7]{2}e^{i2\pi/7}, x = \sqrt[7]{2}e^{i4\pi/7}, \ldots, x = \sqrt[7]{2}e^{i12\pi/7}\text{.} \end{equation*}

This allows us to write down the 9 factors of degree 1 for \(p(x)\) over \(\mathbb C\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} x^7-2 = (x-\sqrt[7]{2})(x-\sqrt[7]{2}e^{i2\pi/7})(x-\sqrt[7]{2}e^{i4\pi/7})\cdots(x-\sqrt[7]{2}e^{i12\pi/7})\text{.} \end{equation*}

Finally, over \(\mathbb R\) we can multiply out each pair of complex conjugate factors. To find them, look at the unit circle. This gives:

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} p(x) = (x+1)(x-3)(x-\sqrt[7]{2})&(x^2 - 2^{8/7}\cos(2\pi/7)x + 2^{2/7})\cdot \\ & \cdot(x^2 - 2^{8/7}\cos(4\pi/7)x + 2^{2/7})(x^2 - 2^{8/7}\cos(6\pi/7)x + 2^{2/7})\end{aligned} \end{equation*}
