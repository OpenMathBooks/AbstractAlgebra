Proposition 2.4.

Let \({\mathbb Z}_n\) be the set of equivalence classes of the integers mod \(n\) and \(a, b, c \in {\mathbb Z}_n\text{.}\)

  1. Addition and multiplication are commutative:

    \begin{align*} a + b & \equiv b + a \pmod{ n}\\ a b & \equiv b a \pmod{ n}\text{.} \end{align*}

  2. Addition and multiplication are associative:

    \begin{align*} (a + b) + c & \equiv a + (b + c)\pmod{ n}\\ (a b) c & \equiv a (b c) \pmod{ n}\text{.} \end{align*}

  3. There are both additive and multiplicative identities:

    \begin{align*} a + 0 & \equiv a \pmod{ n}\\ a \cdot 1 & \equiv a \pmod{ n}\text{.} \end{align*}

  4. Multiplication distributes over addition:

    \begin{equation*} a (b + c) \equiv a b + a c \pmod{ n}\text{.} \end{equation*}

  5. For every integer \(a\) there is an additive inverse \(-a\text{:}\)

    \begin{equation*} a + (-a) \equiv 0 \pmod{ n}\text{.} \end{equation*}

  6. Let \(a\) be a nonzero integer. Then \(\gcd(a,n) = 1\) if and only if there exists a multiplicative inverse \(b\) for \(a \pmod{n}\text{;}\) that is, a nonzero integer \(b\) such that

    \begin{equation*} a b \equiv 1 \pmod{ n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Proof.

We will prove (1) and (6) and leave the remaining properties to be proven in the exercises.

(1) Addition and multiplication are commutative modulo \(n\) since the remainder of \(a + b\) divided by \(n\) is the same as the remainder of \(b + a\) divided by \(n\text{.}\)

(6) Suppose that \(\gcd(a, n) = 1\text{.}\) Then there exist integers \(r\) and \(s\) such that \(ar + ns = 1\text{.}\) Since \(ns = 1 - ar\text{,}\) it must be the case that \(ar \equiv 1 \pmod{n}\text{.}\) Letting \(b\) be the equivalence class of \(r\text{,}\) \(a b \equiv 1\pmod{n}\text{.}\)

Conversely, suppose that there exists an integer \(b\) such that \(ab \equiv 1 \pmod{ n}\text{.}\) Then \(n\) divides \(ab -1\text{,}\) so there is an integer \(k\) such that \(ab - nk = 1\text{.}\) Let \(d = \gcd(a,n)\text{.}\) Since \(d\) divides \(ab - nk\text{,}\) \(d\) must also divide \(1\text{;}\) hence, \(d = 1\text{.}\)
