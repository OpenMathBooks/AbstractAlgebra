1. The only primes which divide \(|U(35)|\) are 2 and 3, so we are looking for a \(2\)-group and a \(3\)-group. We take \(G(2) = \{1,6,8,13,22, 27, 29, 34\}\) to be the group of elements whose orders are powers of \(2\) and \(G(3) = \{1, 11, 16\}\) to be the group of elements whose orders are powers of 3. Clearly \(G(2) \cap G(3) = \{1\}\text{,}\) and we can also show that \(G(2)\cdot G(3) = U(35)\) by simply multiplying out every pair of elements, one from \(G(2)\) and the other from \(G(3)\) (we will get exactly the elements of \(U(35)\)).

  2. \(H = G(2)\text{.}\) We take any element of \(G(2)\) of maximal order. That is, consider \(\langle 8 \rangle = \{1, 8, 29, 22\}\text{.}\) For \(H'\) we need a 2-element subgroup generated by an element not in \(\langle 8\rangle\text{.}\) So let \(H' = \{1, 6\}\text{.}\) Again, the intersection is clearly just \(\{1\}\) and multiplying the elements of \(\langle 8 \rangle\) by \(6\) gives the other elements of \(H\text{.}\)

  3. \(G(3) \cong \Z_{3}\) and \(H' \cong \Z_2\text{.}\) Since \(\langle 8\rangle\) contains an element of order 4 we have \(\langle 8 \rangle \cong \Z_{4}\text{.}\) Thus

    \begin{equation*} U(35) \cong \Z_2 \times \Z_3 \times \Z_4. \end{equation*}
